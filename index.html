Environmental Stratification to Model the Potential Impacts of Climate Change on Giant Panda Habitat in South-Central China

Overview

Modelling the impact of climate change on giant panda habitats is essential in order to develop suitable conservation strategies which mitigate the effects of environmental change. The extinction risk for endangered species threatened by small populations and limited geographic range, may be increased by the effects of climate change (Fordham et al., 2013; Moritz et al., 2008). It can then be assumed that giant panda populations will become increasingly under threat with in a changing climate.

The aim of this project is to analyse the potential impacts of climate change on giant panda habitat based on a statistically derived Global Environmental Stratification (GEnS), using a range of CIMP5 global climate models (GCM) across four representative concentration pathways (RCP).

A supervised classification algorithm (random forests) was used to produce a statistically derived environmental stratification using a set of bioclimatic variables calculated for current climatic conditions and a set of GCMs. The classification procedure produced a set of environmental stratifications (n = 127) consisting of 75 strata which were aggregated into 13 bioclimatic zones. Geospatial analysis techniques were utilised to identify the extent of environmental change over the current panda geographic range in response to climate change.

The analysis indicates that projected climate change may result in significant environmental change to the giant panda’s current geographical range. These impacts are likely to effect the distribution of panda habitat in the near future. Conservation strategies must incorporate possible climate change impacts in order to adapt and move reserves to more suitable areas.

Present Climate Stratification

The current GEnS stratification for the IUCN panda habitat zones consists of 9 bioclimatic zones and 35 environmental strata, ranging from “Extremely cold and wet” to “hot and dry”. Over 80% of the region is characterised by three bioclimatic zones; “Warm temperate and mesic”, “Cold and mesic”, “Cool temperate and moist”. The “warm temperate and mesic” zone is the most common by area, covering 29% of the current panda geographic range.

2050 Climate Stratification

The majority cell statistic results for 2050 (TR, 6.2) highlights the variation in bioclimatic zones associated to panda habitat over each RCP scenario. There are significant changes to the extent of several bioclimatic zones which characterise the current panda habitat. On average, there will be a dramatic decrease in area of the “cold and mesic” zone, which is projected to contract by approximately 55%. Equally, there will a substantial change in the extent of the “warm temperate and mesic” zone which is projected to increase by 55%.

2070 Climate Stratification

Model projections for the 2070 time-slice indicate further north ward movement of bioclimatic zones. In general, there will be a continuation of the movements projected for the 2050 models. “Warm temperate and mesic” zones are estimated to see further reductions in the south and west. RCP 8.5 has the most significant movement of strata, particularly for the ‘cool temperate and moist” bioclimatic zone, which will see significant reductions in the Quiling mountains (northernmost IUCN zone). There will be a further decrease in “cold and mesic” zones, reducing in extent by 66% in comparison to present day conditions.
